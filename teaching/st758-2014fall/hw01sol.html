Q1

Why not test equality with floating point numbers

seven <- .7
two <- .2
one <- .1

Surprised?

1 == (seven + two) + one
## [1] FALSE
1 == seven + (two + one)
## [1] TRUE
1 == (seven + one) + two
## [1] TRUE

Now lets look at the internal representation:

sprintf("%a", c(seven, two, one))       # three numbers
## [1] "0x1.6666666666666p-1" "0x1.999999999999ap-3" "0x1.999999999999ap-4"
  • Binary format of 0.7 is \(1.0110...0110 \times 2^{-1}\)
  • Binary format of 0.2 is \(1.1001...1010 \times 2^{-3}\) (rounding happens here)
  • Binary format of 0.1 is \(1.1001...1010 \times 2^{-4}\) (rounding happens here)
    Lets take a look at the intemediate numbers when doing (0.7 + 0.2) + 0.1.
sprintf("%a", c(seven + two, one))
## [1] "0x1.cccccccccccccp-1" "0x1.999999999999ap-4"
sprintf("%a", (seven + two) + one)
## [1] "0x1.fffffffffffffp-1"

For the partial sum 0.7 + 0.2, computer (1) aligns the exponent, (2) performs addition, and then (3) normalizes the result. So we get \[ \begin{eqnarray*} 1.0110...0110 &\times& 2^{-1} \\ + 0.0110...0110 &\times& 2^{-1} \\ = 1.1100...1100 &\times& 2^{-1} \end{eqnarray*} \] Note in the aligning exponent step, we lost the last two digis of 0.2. Next we add the partial sum to 0.1 \[ \begin{eqnarray*} 1.1100...1100 &\times& 2^{-1} \\ + 0.0011...0011 &\times& 2^{-1} \\ = 1.1111...1111 &\times& 2^{-1} \end{eqnarray*} \] Note again we lost three digits from 0.1 when aligning exponents. The final result is a bit off from 1.

sprintf("%a", 1)                  
## [1] "0x1p+0"

You may work on the other two orders: 0.7 + (0.2 + 0.1) and (0.7 + 0.1) + 0.2, which lead to exact 1.

sprintf("%a", c(seven, two + one))      # partial sum
## [1] "0x1.6666666666666p-1" "0x1.3333333333334p-2"
sprintf("%a", seven + (two + one))        # why it's not one
## [1] "0x1p+0"

(0.7+0.1)+0.2

sprintf("%a", c(seven + one, two))      # partial sum
## [1] "0x1.9999999999999p-1" "0x1.999999999999ap-3"
sprintf("%a", (seven + one) + two)        # why it's not one
## [1] "0x1p+0"

Q3

This is simply the polynomial \(y = (x-1)^7\) expanded. \(y\) values are very close to 0 when \(x\) are close to 0. This calculation is taking differences of very close numbers and losing precision (catastropic cancellation 2). The results dont appear like polynomials due to roundoff errors.

# ggplot2 for nicer graphics
library(ggplot2)
x = seq(from=0.988, to=1.012, by=0.001)
y1 = x^7 - 7 * x^6 + 21 * x^5 - 35 * x^4 + 35 * x^3 - 21 * x^2 + 7 * x -1
qplot(x, y1)

This avoids catastropic cancellation and the result looks much nicer.

y2 = (x - 1)^7
qplot(x, y2)
